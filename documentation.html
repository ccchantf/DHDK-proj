Typography

Style 1: 1500-1800

During the period of 1500-1800, Blackletter, or Gothic script, was mainly used for typography in Europe due to its cultural history and ornateness. Therefore, the font family UnifrakturMaguntia is chosen in style 1 for displaying text content.

For the background, the color of burlywood is used to illustrate the yellowish brown color of old paper. The text color is slightly grey instead of pure black, as if the black printing words were fading.

The texts are well-justified, creating a balanced view as well as the even margins on both the left and the right side.

Margins are wider for the left, right and bottom sides, but narrower for the top margin. This ensures the visual stability of the text block with appropriate negative spaces.

As a characteristics of typography in 1500-1800, the first letter in every paragraph is enlarged and occupies several lines.

It is also narrow and tight for the word and line spacing (line height) as to preserve space.

Style 2: 1970-2000

Libre Baskerville is used in style 2. Between 1970 and 2000, fonts like Serif were still prevalent and commonly used for its high readability.

The dark-blue background color contrasts with the white font color to produce a sharper and more modern visualization effect.

Pages between 1970 and 2000 have a relatively narrower margins on all sides, with a more flexible alignment of texts (but mostly to the left).

Line spacing and word spacing are also wider and sparsed.

Images were displayed with the advancement in technology at that time.
